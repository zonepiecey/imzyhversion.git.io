CHATGPT++

PwC | Enterprise Generative AI | Product Design Lead

PwC ChatGPT++

I designed an enterprise AI workspace that moved ChatGPT++ beyond a simple chat interface into a governed platform for reusable prompts, team collaboration, role-based plugins, and client-specific workspace configuration.

Role Lead Product Designer
Domain Enterprise generative AI, collaboration, workflow tooling
Users Consultants, business users, admins, plugin contributors
Scope Product strategy, IA, key workflows, UI system, handoff

The Problem

Early enterprise GenAI adoption created a familiar pattern: everyone wanted the speed of conversational AI, but teams quickly needed more than an open text box. Users needed reusable prompts, trusted plugins, team spaces, admin controls, and client-specific configuration. Without structure, the product risked becoming a scattered collection of chats, templates, and experimental AI tools.

The design challenge was to preserve the immediacy of chat while introducing the control, reuse, and governance expected in a professional services environment.

01

Chat alone did not scale

Users could generate useful outputs, but valuable workflows were hard to repeat, share, version, or standardize across teams.

02

AI needed business context

Prompts, examples, files, plugins, and client/team settings had to become visible inputs rather than hidden user behavior.

03

Governance had to feel usable

Role permissions, plugin modes, and team access controls were necessary, but they had to fit naturally into the flow of work.

Product Strategy

I reframed ChatGPT++ as a layered workspace with four connected surfaces: private productivity, team collaboration, reusable templates, and a plugin ecosystem. This gave the product a clearer mental model: users could start with a simple chat, then add structured prompts, activate business plugins, and move work into team spaces when collaboration or governance mattered.

The design principle was progressive structure. Simple tasks should still feel fast, while higher-stakes workflows should reveal the right controls at the right moment.

Onboarding and Workspace Setup

The first-time experience needed to explain that ChatGPT++ could be customized for a client's operating context. I designed onboarding around configuration tasks that matter in enterprise deployment: login, client information, branding, domain setup, and initial workspace entry.

This turned onboarding from a generic welcome screen into a product setup flow. The goal was to make the platform feel ownable by each team while keeping the setup lightweight enough for business users.

Information Architecture

The navigation model separated personal work, team work, templates, plugins, documents, and support. This was important because enterprise AI work moves between modes: a user may draft privately, reuse a template, invoke a plugin, share with a team, or manage access for others.

I used the side navigation as the product's spine. It gave users a stable map while allowing each team or domain to expose its own assistants, templates, and shared workspaces.

Reusable Prompt Templates

One of the biggest opportunities was turning good prompt behavior into a reusable product pattern. Instead of asking every user to learn prompt engineering, the template builder translated prompt structure into guided fields: context, purpose, AI role, tone, required format, example files, plugins, and confirmation.

This reduced the gap between expert users and everyday business users. It also made prompts easier to review, share, and govern because the important ingredients were explicit.

Plugin Ecosystem

Plugins were the bridge between general-purpose chat and business-specific action. I designed the plugin library around two distinct modes: consumers who install and use approved plugins, and contributors who create, update, and submit plugin assets.

This role split was critical. Business users needed a simple store-like discovery experience, while contributors needed versioning, status, and clearer publishing controls. Treating those as different experiences helped the platform support scale without overwhelming either audience.

Workflow Example

To test how plugins should behave inside the chat experience, I designed an earning transcript workflow. The plugin guided users through file upload, structured instructions, and continuation back to chat. This pattern clarified that plugins were not static cards; they were task-specific extensions of the conversation.

Team Collaboration

Enterprise AI work often becomes collaborative once it touches a client, a delivery team, or a shared knowledge domain. I designed team spaces so users could create group chats, manage membership, assign roles, and control template access without leaving the product's core environment.

The team model helped ChatGPT++ move from individual experimentation to shared operational use. It also created a foundation for permissioning, shared assistants, and domain-specific AI resources.

Key Design Decisions

1. Keep chat as the anchor, not the whole product

The chat interface remained the fastest path to value, but templates, plugins, documents, and team spaces gave it enterprise structure.

2. Turn prompt engineering into guided composition

Template fields made high-quality prompting repeatable and reviewable, reducing dependence on individual prompt-writing skill.

3. Split plugin consumption from plugin contribution

Different users needed different affordances. Consumers needed trust and discovery; contributors needed publishing, versioning, and lifecycle states.

4. Make governance part of the workflow

Roles, permissions, upload settings, plugin states, and team access were surfaced where decisions happened instead of hidden in settings.

Outcome

The final design gave ChatGPT++ a scalable product architecture: private and team workspaces, guided templates, plugin discovery, contributor workflows, contextual plugin triggers, client customization, and access management. It turned a generic GenAI interaction into a professional workspace that could support repeatable business use cases.

The strongest design outcome was not only the individual screens. It was the operating model: ChatGPT++ could now absorb new templates, plugins, client configurations, and team structures without asking users to relearn the product every time.

Reusable AI work

Converted one-off prompts into templates and plugin-assisted workflows that teams could repeat and share.

Enterprise trust

Made role, permission, publishing, and plugin status decisions visible in the main product experience.

Platform scale

Created a modular structure for adding new clients, domains, teams, templates, and plugins over time.
